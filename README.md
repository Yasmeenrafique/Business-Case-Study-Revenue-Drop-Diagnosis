#  Business Case Study: Revenue Drop Diagnosis


**Topic:** Business Case  Diagnosing a Revenue Drop  
**Industry:** eCommerce — Fashion / Shopify  

---

## Business Scenario

A Shopify fashion brand based in Lahore has been operating for 2 years. In the most recent month, revenue dropped from **Rs. 2,200,000 to Rs. 1,400,000** — a **36% decline**.

The CEO reports:
- Marketing Manager says ads are performing fine
- Operations Manager says stock levels are fine
- Finance Manager sees nothing unusual

---

## My Analysis

###  Clarifying Questions

Before touching any data, I asked the CEO the following questions:

1. **Has anything changed in the last 30 days?**
   A new supplier, delivery partner, price increase, website update, or team change? A sudden drop on one specific date points to a single event. A gradual drop across the month points to a systemic problem.

2. **Did the drop happen suddenly or gradually?**
   Sudden = likely one specific cause (website crash, bad review, payment gateway failure).
   Gradual = likely an experience or retention problem building over time.

3. **Is this month seasonally slower than normal?**
   Fashion is seasonal. Post-Eid or off-peak months naturally see lower sales. External factors must be eliminated before investigating internal ones.

---

###  Hypothesis

Using the eCommerce revenue formula:

```
Revenue = Traffic × Conversion Rate × AOV × Repeat Purchase Rate
```

Possible causes of the 36% drop:

| Hypothesis | Revenue Lever Affected |
|---|---|
| Ads attracting wrong audience | Traffic quality |
| Website UI/UX issues at checkout | Conversion Rate |
| Product prices too high vs competitors | AOV / Conversion Rate |
| Late delivery or damaged packaging | Repeat Purchase Rate |
| Poor customer service post-purchase | Repeat Purchase Rate |
| Seasonal slowdown | All levers |

---

### Customer Journey Diagnosis

I mapped the drop against the 5-stage customer journey:

```
Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Experience → Retention
```

| Stage | Assessment | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Not broken | Store was generating strong revenue before — traffic was coming in |
| Consideration | Not broken | Customers were previously converting well — trust was established |
| Purchase | Monitor closely | Check if cart abandonment rate increased this specific month |
| **Experience** | **Main suspect** | Something changed in delivery, packaging, or customer service this month |
| **Retention** | **Consequence** | Bad experience killed repeat purchase rate — loyal customers stopped returning |

> **Root cause hypothesis:** Something in the Experience stage changed this month — a new courier partner, a supplier quality issue, or a drop in customer service response times.

---

###  Data Requested From Client

I requested the following data covering the last 60 days vs the previous 60 days:

**Sales Data**
- Daily order volume (to identify if drop was sudden or gradual)
- Revenue by product category
- Average Order Value by week

**Customer Behaviour Data**
- Conversion rate by week
- Cart abandonment rate
- Repeat purchase rate
- New vs returning customer ratio

**Operations Data**
- Average delivery time by courier partner
- Return rate by product category
- Customer service ticket volume and response times
- Product packaging damage reports or complaints

**Customer Feedback**
- Reviews and ratings from the last 60 days
- Any spike in negative reviews mentioning delivery or quality

---

###  CEO Presentation 

> Your store has been performing strongly for 2 years. This month's 36% revenue drop is significant — but the pattern tells an important story.
>
> Your Awareness and Consideration stages are not broken. Customers were still finding you and trusting you. The problem lives in what happened **after** they bought.
>
> Our data shows a spike in delivery complaints and a sharp drop in repeat purchases this month. Customers who previously returned to buy again simply stopped. One bad experience — whether late delivery, damaged packaging, or unanswered support queries — is enough to lose a loyal customer permanently.
>
> **What we recommend:**
> 1. Audit your courier partner performance for this month — compare delivery times against your SLA
> 2. Review customer service response times — identify if tickets went unanswered
> 3. Reach out to the customers who had bad experiences with a personal apology and a discount code
>
> **What this is worth:**
> If we recover even 30% of the customers lost this month through a retention campaign, that is approximately Rs. 420,000 in recovered revenue — without spending a single extra rupee on ads.
>
> If we do nothing, these customers will not return, and your repeat purchase rate will continue falling month over month.

## Key Concepts Applied

| Applications |

| Revenue formula | Used to identify which lever was affected |
| Cost structure | Understood that retention is 10x cheaper than acquisition |
| Customer journey | Mapped the drop to Experience and Retention stages |
| Diagnostic thinking | Ruled out external causes before internal diagnosis |
| Stakeholder communication | Presented findings in business language, not technical language |


---

## Lessons Learned

- **Always check seasonality first** before assuming an internal problem
- **Clarifying questions save hours** of analysis on the wrong problem
- **Sudden vs gradual drop** is the first diagnostic split — it changes everything
- **Experience stage is invisible on most dashboards** but causes the most damage
- **Retention is a consequence** — you cannot fix it without fixing Experience first
- **Every recommendation needs a number** — clients act on rupees, not advice
